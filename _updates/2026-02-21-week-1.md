After federal deregulation took hold in January, states are moving aggressively to fill the regulatory void. This week saw multiple proposals and a clear signal from the Trump administration that it expects industry to lead, not government.

## 1. Colorado Proposes AI Transparency and Testing Requirements

**The news:** Colorado House Bill 1299 would require developers of "high-impact AI systems" (used in hiring, lending, benefits, housing) to:
- Disclose training data sources
- Conduct bias and fairness testing
- Make results available to consumers
- Report to the Colorado Attorney General

**Why it matters:** If passed, Colorado would join California and New York as a state enforcing AI safety standards. Companies operating nationwide may face de facto national regulation if three large states impose similar rules.

**Status:** In committee. Expected vote March 2026.

---

## 2. Massachusetts Debates AI Safety Testing Mandate

**The news:** The Massachusetts legislature is reviewing a proposal that would require "large generative AI models" to undergo safety certification before deployment. The proposal mirrors parts of California's failed SB 1047.

**Why it matters:** Massachusetts is a major tech hub (Boston, Cambridge). A strict rule here could influence hiring and model development decisions industry-wide.

**Key debate:** State officials vs. tech companies. State says "safety first"; tech says "innovation will move to other states."

**Status:** Hearing scheduled for March 2026.

---

## 3. Trump Administration Signals Preemption Concerns

**The news:** In a memo to federal agencies, the Office of Management and Budget cautioned agencies to avoid "rules that conflict with state-level AI freedom." The memo doesn't prohibit state regulation but signals the administration's view: **states should not impose burdensome AI rules.**

**Why it matters:** This is a shot across the bow to California, Colorado, and other states considering strict AI regulation. It signals potential legal challenges if states impose rules deemed to interfere with interstate commerce.

**Legal question:** Can the Commerce Clause preempt state AI regulation? This remains unresolved.

---

## 4. EU's AI Act Enters Enforcement Phase

**The news:** The European Union began enforcing the AI Act's first wave of rules. Developers of high-risk AI systems must now demonstrate compliance with safety testing, documentation, and bias mitigation requirements.

**Early reports:** Compliance costs are substantial. A startup survey found average compliance spend of $500K–$2M for the first year. Some companies report 6–18 month delays in model releases.

**Why it matters:** This is the world's strictest AI regulation in practice. If it works (reduces harms without killing innovation), other jurisdictions may follow. If it stifles innovation, it will be cited as a cautionary tale.

**Impact on U.S. companies:** Firms serving EU customers must comply. This creates a de facto global standard (companies tend to build to the strictest rule they face).

---

## 5. China Accelerates Domestic AI Development

**The news:** Chinese AI labs published multiple new models this week, including advances in reasoning and multimodal capabilities. Meanwhile, the Chinese government approved new funding for AI research and development.

**Why it matters:** As the U.S. shifts to deregulation and the EU imposes strict rules, China is building AI unconstrained by safety regulations or international alignment. This raises competitive and security concerns.

**Geopolitical context:** The U.S. is now in a three-way AI governance and development race: compete with China, avoid the EU's strict rules, and navigate internal fragmentation.

---

## Analysis: The State vs. Federal Stalemate

The clear pattern this week: **the U.S. is entering a regulatory stalemate.**

- **States** are moving because the federal government has stepped back (Trump's deregulation)
- **The federal government** wants states to step back because uniform regulation is easier for business
- **Tech companies** want federal preemption, but they're getting neither federal rules nor permission to ignore states

**The likely outcome:** A multi-year fight. Either:
1. The federal government establishes preemption authority and writes national AI rules (unlikely under Trump), or
2. States win and companies adapt to a patchwork of rules (expensive but survivable), or
3. A compromise emerges where federal law sets a floor and states can exceed it (like environmental regulation)

**For developers:** Build with compliance in mind. Assume California, Colorado, Massachusetts, and New York rules will apply to your system if you want to operate nationwide.
