# A History of AI Governance

From algorithmic bias in hiring systems to the explosive growth of large language models, artificial intelligence has reshaped how governments, companies, and citizens think about regulation. This timeline traces the evolution of AI policy from the pre-generative AI era to today's fragmented regulatory landscape.

<div class="timeline">

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2016–2019</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">2016–2019</div>
<div class="timeline-title">Early AI Regulation & Algorithmic Bias Concerns</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
Before ChatGPT, AI governance was scattered and nascent. The <strong>GDPR</strong> (General Data Protection Regulation, effective May 2018) became Europe's foundational AI regulation framework, introducing "right to explanation" requirements for automated decisions. In the U.S., concerns about algorithmic bias in hiring, lending, and criminal justice mounted. The FDA began reviewing AI/ML algorithms used in medical devices. The focus was narrow: preventing discrimination and ensuring transparency, not managing general-purpose AI systems.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-medium">Foundational</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">2019–2021</div>
<div class="timeline-title">AI Strategy Documents, Modest Enforcement</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
The EU published the <strong>AI Act</strong> proposal (April 2021), introducing a risk-based tiered approach: banned high-risk systems (biometric identification), restricted medium-risk systems (hiring algorithms), and lightly regulated low-risk systems (chatbots). The U.S. took a lighter approach: NIST published the "AI Risk Management Framework," the Office of Management and Budget proposed algorithmic accountability guidance, but enforcement was minimal. China published <strong>AI Ethics Guidelines</strong> and invested heavily in AI research. The assumption was that self-regulation by tech companies would suffice.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-medium">Strategic</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2022</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">November 2022</div>
<div class="timeline-title">ChatGPT Launch — The Inflection Point</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
<strong>OpenAI released ChatGPT to the public.</strong> The model achieved 100 million users in 2 months — faster than any tech product in history. Suddenly, "AI" wasn't abstract. Governments, regulators, and the public confronted a real system that could write essays, answer questions, and generate code. The regulatory landscape, designed for narrow algorithmic systems, faced a new creature: a general-purpose AI system accessible to anyone. The era of aggressive AI governance began.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-critical">Catalyst</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2023</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">2023</div>
<div class="timeline-title">Global AI Safety Panic & Regulatory Scramble</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
Following ChatGPT's success, Claude, Bard, and other LLMs reached mainstream adoption. AI safety concerns dominated headlines: could these systems cause harm? Were they sentient? Could they pose existential risks? The <strong>UK</strong> hosted the AI Safety Summit (November 2023) with 28 nations, focusing on frontier AI risks. The <strong>EU finalized the AI Act</strong> (June 2023), the world's most comprehensive AI regulation. <strong>China</strong> published generative AI rules restricting "harmful content." <strong>U.S. regulators</strong> held hearings with OpenAI's Sam Altman, but the federal government remained fragmented. States began acting independently.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-critical">Regulatory Surge</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2024</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">October 2024</div>
<div class="timeline-title">Biden Executive Order on AI</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
<strong>President Joe Biden signed Executive Order 14110 on Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy Artificial Intelligence.</strong> The order directed federal agencies to establish AI safety standards, conduct algorithmic impact assessments, and mitigate bias in AI systems. It required the NIST to develop AI safety benchmarks and authorized the Department of Commerce to regulate AI chip exports (targeting China). The order was industry-friendly: it encouraged innovation while setting guardrails for high-risk domains (healthcare, law enforcement). It <strong>did not</strong> establish a federal AI regulator or ban any AI systems. Compliance was delegated to agencies, creating fragmentation.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-high">Federal Action</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">2024 (Ongoing)</div>
<div class="timeline-title">California's SB 1047 — The Preemption Fight</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
<strong>California's Senate Bill 1047</strong> would have required AI developers to conduct safety testing before deploying large language models. It also proposed to create a state attorney general's office for AI oversight. The bill passed the legislature but Governor Gavin Newsom <strong>vetoed it</strong> (September 2024), citing concerns about chilling innovation and the federal government's preemption authority.

However, two follow-up bills passed: <strong>SB 1091</strong> (Transparency) requires AI systems used in education to disclose their use. <strong>AB 701</strong> (Deepfakes) restricts non-consensual intimate images generated by AI.

The veto sparked national debate: <strong>Should states or the federal government regulate AI?</strong> Can California set AI standards that effectively become national? Tech companies argued federal preemption is necessary; consumer advocates argued states must fill the vacuum left by federal inaction.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-high">Jurisdictional Clash</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">2024–2025</div>
<div class="timeline-title">International Divergence</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
AI regulation is fracturing along geopolitical lines. <strong>EU:</strong> The AI Act (effective from 2024–2026) imposes the world's strictest rules — high-risk systems face rigorous compliance testing, generative AI must disclose training data, penalties up to 6% of global revenue. <strong>China:</strong> Generative AI Regulations require models to align with "socialist values" and undergo security review. <strong>UK:</strong> Post-Brexit, the UK adopted a pro-innovation, light-touch approach using existing laws rather than AI-specific regulation. <strong>U.S.:</strong> Fragmented between federal deregulation and state-level rules, with no overarching framework.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-high">Global Fragmentation</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2025</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">January 2025</div>
<div class="timeline-title">Trump Takes Office, EO Reversal Begins</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
President Donald Trump signed <strong>Executive Order 14131 on Reducing Regulatory Burdens of AI</strong> (January 2025). The order rescinded Biden's EO and redirected federal policy: reduce "regulatory burdens," promote American AI innovation, and compete globally with China. It dissolved the AI Safety Institute and redirected NIST resources toward innovation metrics rather than safety standards. Federal agencies were ordered to reduce AI regulations. The immediate impact: reduced oversight of AI development, clarity withdrawn, and responsibility shifted to states and industry self-regulation.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-critical">Deregulation</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">February 2025</div>
<div class="timeline-title">State vs. Federal Regulatory War Heats Up</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
With federal deregulation underway, states moved to fill the gap: <strong>Colorado</strong> proposed AI transparency and bias testing requirements. <strong>New York</strong> expanded its AI auditing requirements beyond hiring. <strong>Massachusetts</strong> debated AI safety testing mandates. <strong>Washington State</strong> considered algorithmic accountability laws.

Meanwhile, the Trump administration signaled that states cannot impose burdensome AI regulations if they conflict with federal commerce and innovation goals. This set the stage for a potential constitutional showdown over the Commerce Clause and state authority.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-high">Ongoing Tension</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2026</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">February 2026 (Present)</div>
<div class="timeline-title">The Current Landscape</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
As of February 2026, AI governance is at a crossroads. <strong>In Europe:</strong> The AI Act is being implemented with significant compliance costs. <strong>In the U.S.:</strong> Federal deregulation is underway while states race to regulate. Tech companies face uncertainty about whether federal preemption will protect them or whether they'll face 50 different sets of rules. <strong>In China:</strong> AI development is tightly controlled but accelerating.

<strong>Key questions unanswered:</strong> Will the U.S. unify under federal regulation or remain a patchwork? Can Europe's strict approach coexist with the U.S. regulatory void? How will frontier AI risks be governed in a fragmented environment? The era of unregulated AI is ending — the question is how and where regulation will happen.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-critical">Ongoing</span>
</div>

</div>

## Why This Matters

If you're building AI systems, regulatory clarity has become a business concern:

1. **Know your audience.** A model compliant with the EU AI Act might not work in China. A California-compliant system might not operate nationwide.
2. **Plan for compliance costs.** Testing, documentation, and auditing are now expected.
3. **Watch the U.S. federal-state fight.** The outcome will determine whether companies face a patchwork of rules or eventual unified clarity.
4. **Understand your liability.** As regulation firms up, enforcement actions and lawsuits will follow.
5. **Engage with policy.** The regulatory landscape is still forming. Industry input during rulemaking can shape outcomes.
