<div class="page-header">
  <h1>A History of AI Governance</h1>
  <p>From algorithmic bias in hiring systems to the explosive growth of large language models, artificial intelligence has reshaped how governments, companies, and citizens think about regulation. This timeline traces the evolution of AI policy from the pre-generative AI era to today's fragmented regulatory landscape.</p>
</div>

<div class="timeline">

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2016–2019</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">2016–2019</div>
<div class="timeline-title">Early AI Regulation & Algorithmic Bias Concerns</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
Before ChatGPT, AI governance was scattered and nascent. The <strong>GDPR</strong> (General Data Protection Regulation, effective May 2018) became Europe's foundational data protection framework with AI-relevant provisions, including transparency requirements for automated decision-making under Articles 13–15 and 22. In the U.S., concerns about algorithmic bias in hiring, lending, and criminal justice mounted. The FDA began reviewing AI/ML algorithms used in medical devices, with dozens approved by 2019. The focus was narrow: preventing discrimination and ensuring transparency, not managing general-purpose AI systems.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-medium">Foundational</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">2019–2021</div>
<div class="timeline-title">AI Strategy Documents, Modest Enforcement</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
The EU published the <strong>AI Act</strong> proposal (April 2021), introducing a risk-based tiered approach with four categories: prohibited AI practices (such as social scoring), high-risk systems (biometric identification, hiring algorithms) requiring rigorous compliance, limited-risk systems (chatbots) with transparency obligations, and minimal-risk systems with no restrictions. The U.S. took a lighter approach: the Office of Management and Budget proposed algorithmic accountability guidance, but enforcement was minimal. China published <strong>AI Ethics Guidelines</strong> and invested heavily in AI research. The assumption was that self-regulation by tech companies would suffice.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-medium">Strategic</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2022</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">November 2022</div>
<div class="timeline-title">ChatGPT Launch — The Inflection Point</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
<strong>OpenAI released ChatGPT to the public.</strong> The model achieved 100 million users in roughly 2 months — faster than any consumer application in history. Suddenly, "AI" wasn't abstract. Governments, regulators, and the public confronted a real system that could write essays, answer questions, and generate code. The regulatory landscape, designed for narrow algorithmic systems, faced a new creature: a general-purpose AI system accessible to anyone. The era of aggressive AI governance began.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-critical">Catalyst</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2023</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">2023</div>
<div class="timeline-title">Global AI Safety Panic & Regulatory Scramble</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
Following ChatGPT's success, Claude, Bard, and other LLMs reached mainstream adoption. AI safety concerns dominated headlines. The <strong>UK</strong> hosted the AI Safety Summit at Bletchley Park (November 2023) with 28 countries and the EU, focusing on frontier AI risks and producing the Bletchley Declaration. The <strong>European Parliament adopted its position on the AI Act</strong> (June 2023), with the final political agreement between Parliament and Council reached in December 2023. <strong>China</strong> published Interim Administrative Measures for Generative AI Services (effective August 2023). <strong>U.S. regulators</strong> held hearings with OpenAI's Sam Altman, and President Biden signed <strong>Executive Order 14110</strong> on Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy AI (October 30, 2023). NIST published the <strong>AI Risk Management Framework</strong> (AI RMF 1.0, January 2023).
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-critical">Regulatory Surge</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2024</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">2024</div>
<div class="timeline-title">Biden Executive Order Takes Effect, EU AI Act Published</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
<strong>Executive Order 14110</strong> (signed October 30, 2023) directed federal agencies to establish AI safety standards, conduct impact assessments, and mitigate bias in AI systems. It required NIST to develop AI safety benchmarks and set new requirements for federal AI procurement via OMB Memorandum M-24-18. The order was industry-friendly: it encouraged innovation while setting guardrails for high-risk domains (healthcare, law enforcement). It <strong>did not</strong> establish a federal AI regulator or ban any AI systems. Separately, the Biden administration pursued AI chip export controls through the Bureau of Industry and Security, targeting China. The <strong>EU AI Act</strong> was formally published in the Official Journal on July 12, 2024, entering into force on August 1, 2024.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-high">Federal Action</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">September 2024</div>
<div class="timeline-title">California's SB 1047 — The Preemption Fight</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
<strong>California's Senate Bill 1047</strong> (the Safe and Secure Innovation for Frontier Artificial Intelligence Models Act) would have required AI developers to conduct safety testing before deploying frontier models. It proposed creating a Frontier Model Division under the Department of Technology, with enforcement authority for the Attorney General. The bill passed both chambers of the legislature but Governor Gavin Newsom <strong>vetoed it</strong> (September 29, 2024), citing concerns that the bill applied stringent standards too broadly and failed to account for whether an AI system operates in high-risk environments.

Several related California AI bills did pass in 2024, including <strong>AB 2655</strong> (restricting election deepfakes), <strong>SB 926</strong> (expanding revenge porn laws to AI-generated content), and <strong>AB 2839</strong> (requiring disclosure of AI-generated election content).

The veto sparked national debate: <strong>Should states or the federal government regulate AI?</strong>
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-high">Jurisdictional Clash</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">2024–2025</div>
<div class="timeline-title">International Divergence</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
AI regulation is fracturing along geopolitical lines. <strong>EU:</strong> The AI Act (phased enforcement from 2024–2026) imposes the world's strictest rules — high-risk systems face rigorous compliance testing, providers of general-purpose AI must publish a sufficiently detailed summary of training data, penalties up to 7% of global annual turnover or EUR 35 million for the most serious violations. <strong>China:</strong> Generative AI regulations require models to align with "Core Socialist Values" and undergo security review. <strong>UK:</strong> Post-Brexit, the UK adopted a pro-innovation, light-touch approach using existing regulators and non-statutory principles rather than AI-specific legislation. <strong>U.S.:</strong> Fragmented between federal deregulation and state-level rules, with no overarching framework.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-high">Global Fragmentation</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2025</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">January 2025</div>
<div class="timeline-title">Trump Takes Office, EO Reversal Begins</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
President Donald Trump signed <strong>Executive Order 14179, "Removing Barriers to American Leadership in Artificial Intelligence"</strong> (January 23, 2025). Biden's EO 14110 was rescinded on January 20, 2025 via a separate executive order (EO 14148). Federal policy was redirected: reduce "regulatory burdens," promote American AI innovation, and compete globally with China. The AI Safety Institute at NIST was effectively gutted through staff cuts in February 2025 and later rebranded as the Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) with a fundamentally different mission. Federal agencies were directed to reduce AI regulations. The immediate impact: reduced federal oversight, clarity withdrawn, and responsibility shifted to states and industry self-regulation.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-critical">Deregulation</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">2025</div>
<div class="timeline-title">State vs. Federal Regulatory War Heats Up</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
With federal deregulation underway, states moved to fill the gap. <strong>Colorado</strong> had already signed the Consumer Protections for Artificial Intelligence Act (SB 24-205) into law in May 2024 — the first comprehensive state AI bias law — with its implementation deadline later delayed to June 30, 2026 by SB 25B-004. <strong>New York</strong> expanded its AI auditing requirements beyond hiring. <strong>Massachusetts</strong> debated AI safety testing mandates.

In December 2025, the Trump administration signed an executive order on "Ensuring a National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence," creating a DOJ AI Litigation Task Force to challenge state AI laws in federal court. This set the stage for a potential constitutional showdown over the Commerce Clause and state authority.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-high">Ongoing Tension</span>
</div>

<div class="timeline-year">
  <div class="timeline-year-label">2026</div>
</div>

<div class="timeline-event">
<div class="timeline-date">Early 2026 (Present)</div>
<div class="timeline-title">The Current Landscape</div>
<div class="timeline-description">
AI governance is at a crossroads. <strong>In Europe:</strong> The AI Act's prohibited practices provisions took effect in February 2025, with high-risk system obligations set to begin August 2, 2026. Compliance costs are mounting. <strong>In the U.S.:</strong> Federal deregulation is underway while states race to regulate. Tech companies face uncertainty about whether federal preemption will protect them or whether they'll face 50 different sets of rules. <strong>In China:</strong> AI development is tightly controlled but accelerating, with a wave of major model releases in early 2026.

<strong>Key questions unanswered:</strong> Will the U.S. unify under federal regulation or remain a patchwork? Can Europe's strict approach coexist with the U.S. regulatory void? How will frontier AI risks be governed in a fragmented environment? The era of unregulated AI is ending — the question is how and where regulation will happen.
</div>
<span class="timeline-impact impact-critical">Ongoing</span>
</div>

</div>

<div class="content-section">
  <h2>Why This Matters</h2>
  <p>If you're building AI systems, regulatory clarity has become a business concern:</p>
  <ol>
    <li><strong>Know your audience.</strong> A model compliant with the EU AI Act might not work in China. A California-compliant system might not operate nationwide.</li>
    <li><strong>Plan for compliance costs.</strong> Testing, documentation, and auditing are now expected.</li>
    <li><strong>Watch the U.S. federal-state fight.</strong> The outcome will determine whether companies face a patchwork of rules or eventual unified clarity.</li>
    <li><strong>Understand your liability.</strong> As regulation firms up, enforcement actions and lawsuits will follow.</li>
    <li><strong>Engage with policy.</strong> The regulatory landscape is still forming. Industry input during rulemaking can shape outcomes.</li>
  </ol>
</div>
